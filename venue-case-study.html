🌸 Venue

Increasing retention without adding friction or moderation cost.

Design Studios · UX Case Study — Venue by Yummy Labs

Project
Venue, Design Studios (Phase 2 Social)
Role
Lead UX & Product Designer
Studio
Independent, in collaboration with Yummy Labs / Superbloom
Deliverables
Concept system · Discovery flow · Interaction model · Presentation deck
Constraint
Zero new behaviour required from the player

The Problem

Every night, Beth closes Venue and opens Facebook.

She's been playing for months. She designs rooms, submits them to be voted on, collects rewards. She's engaged. She's good at it. But when she wants to feel something — to share a result, to see if anyone noticed — the game has nothing to offer. So she goes elsewhere.

The problem wasn't low engagement. Venue had 4M+ downloads, a 90% female player base, and strong daily loops.

The problem was that none of it felt like it mattered to anyone else. She had already found her community. She'd just found it outside the product.

📸 Facebook community screenshot vs. Venue in-game social feed — contrasting where Beth's social energy actually goes

The Insight

Most social features in games solve the wrong problem. They ask players to do something social: join a guild, add friends, post a score. But players like Beth don't want to perform belonging. They want to feel it.

Two things follow from that:

1. The moment you ask someone to join, you create a moment where they can say no. Opt-in mechanics are opt-out risks. Every explicit step leaks.

2. Social mechanics that rely on moderation or settings to stay non-toxic will eventually fail. Toxicity needs to be structurally impossible, not just manageable.

The Design Challenge

Yummy Labs' brief: design a group-based social system for Venue that encourages collaboration, creates shared goals, and feels cozy, not competitive.

Not a guild war system. A creative collective.

Three hard constraints made this interesting:

Voting in Venue is anonymous — players can't choose whose work they see. The player base skews casual — no tolerance for extra homework. The game has no chat — no text, no moderation overhead.

📸 Yummy Labs brief slide — "This is not a guild war system. This is a creative collective."

Design Principles

Before touching a single screen, I defined four non-negotiables that every mechanic had to pass.

1

Structurally incapable of toxicity

If a mechanic can produce a bad feeling, it will. The fix isn't a setting — it's removing the mechanism. No leaderboards. No individual contribution visibility. No way to compare members.

2

Absence must feel inconsequential

Missing a week should carry zero guilt signal. No "you let your team down." No streak breaks. Contribution is meaningful when you show up, invisible when you don't.

3

Belonging is observed, not declared

The system watches what players already do and uses it as contribution. Normal play is participation. No opt-in threshold, no confirmation modal.

4

Collective over individual

Milestones are collective. Rewards are shared. Progress is one bar, not aggregated personal bars. The Studio succeeds together or not at all.

The Solution · Design Studios

Design Studios is a passive community membership system embedded in Venue's existing game loop. Players contribute to a shared Studio by playing normally — submitting designs, voting, completing chapters. No new behaviour required.

Each Studio has 10–20 members matched on design aesthetic, a shared weekly goal, a Studio Room that grows as the Studio levels up, and a collective gallery where members' submitted designs appear for each other.

The Studio is named and curated. Ours: Casa Bloom 🌸

Design Precedents · What We Borrowed and Why

Every structural constraint in Design Studios — no leaderboard, no guilt for absence, no free text — has a precedent in a game that already solved it. We didn't invent caution. We borrowed proven architecture.

🧟
State of Decay 2
The volunteering state
In State of Decay 2, you volunteer into another player's survival world as a temporary collaborator. You help, you contribute, you leave — with no permanent record of who did what. No formal commitment required. This is the direct model for how Beth enters a Studio: she plays as normal, contributes passively, and membership becomes true after two weeks without her ever having to decide. The mechanic is structurally incapable of producing rejection.
✨
Journey (thatgamecompany)
No toxicity by architecture
Journey's players meet anonymously, communicate only through a single sound, and never know who their companion was until the credits roll. The game doesn't rely on community guidelines to prevent harassment — it removes the tools for it entirely. This is the foundational principle behind feedback tags and gallery reactions: preset signals only, no free text, no way to send a negative signal even if you wanted to.
🐱
Neko Atsume
Absence without guilt
Neko Atsume has no streak, no timer, no record of what you missed. Cats come and go; the experience is always positive when you return. This is the direct model for how Studios handles absence: the weekly goal advances or it doesn't, but Beth's absence is never made visible, never counted, never punished.

The Joining Mechanic · "You Can't Fail to Belong"

This was the most consequential decision in the entire system.

During onboarding, the player taps a Studio and goes back to play. Nothing changes on screen. But behind the scenes she knows, everything she already does starts contributing to something shared.

Two weekly cycles later, she's a member. Not because she declared it. Because the system observed it. The transition is quiet: a status line change. A private notification. No announcement to the Studio. No fanfare.

Belonging isn't a step. It's a pattern. The moment you ask someone to commit, you surface their ambivalence. This system bypasses that entirely.

From volunteer to member, without crossing a line

In the first two weeks, Beth is technically a volunteer. She contributes through normal play, exactly as members do, but she carries no label, no badge, no expectation. The word "volunteer" never appears in the interface — it's a system-side concept only.

The distinction matters for one reason: it means no one can fail to join. There is no threshold she can miss, no week she can fall short of, no moment where the game decides she hasn't done enough. She simply plays, and the system watches.

After two weekly cycles of contribution, the transition happens automatically. By the time she knows she's a member, she already acts like one.

Language note: The CTA throughout is "Play with this Studio", never "Join Studio." The player is choosing to play, not to commit. That single word swap is load-bearing.

📸 Side-by-side — traditional "Join" CTA flow vs. Design Studios' volunteer-to-member quiet transition

Two Currencies, Two Timelines

The system runs on two currencies that operate on different clocks — and that distinction does most of the retention work.

🔑
Weekly progression
Key
Unlocks the weekly reward tiers: Spark → Bloom → Glow → Radiant. Resets every week. Earned automatically through normal play: submit a design (+1), complete a vote session (+1), earn a 5-star (+2).
This week. Effort that matters now.
↑
Permanent progression
Arrow
Accumulates across weeks and never resets. Earned exclusively by completing the weekly collective challenge as a Studio. Each Arrow level unlocks a new shared Studio Room — permanently.
Forever. Leaving means leaving behind a space she helped build.

How does Beth first encounter her Studio? Not through a banner. Not a push notification. Through a milestone.

At Day 7, Beth has completed several chapters and been through the voting loop many times. The leaderboard just unlocked. She's reward-loop trained — red dots work on her. The trigger: 10 designs submitted. Emotional peak. She's claiming a reward when the system opens a door.

Making Anonymous Voting Feel Human

Voting in Venue is anonymous by design. Players can't choose whose work they see — the algorithm decides. This meant the Studio feed and the voting pool are structurally separate. I couldn't build connection through votes. So I built it elsewhere.

Feedback tags: When Beth votes, she can leave a preset tag alongside her vote. "Great composition." "Beautiful palette." Not a score — a signal. For a few seconds, she's not optimizing a result. She's honoring someone.

Gallery reactions: In the Studio gallery, she can react to her mates' designs with preset emoji responses. No text. No moderation required. But suddenly, recognition is possible between people who know each other.

Zero moderation overhead. Structurally safe. Emotionally meaningful. The preset palette is the design decision — not a limitation.

📸 Voting UI — feedback tag selector, preset tags only, no free text field

Milestones · When the Studio Responds

When the Studio hits a collective threshold, the space responds. Light. Subtle. Shared.

No big celebration. No individual callout. Just enough to say: you were part of this.

Milestone reveal follows a Progressive Reveal model. Rather than a single dramatic reveal that peaks and fades, the Studio room gradually comes to life as the week progresses. This produces steadier sustained engagement and avoids the emotional crash of a big reveal followed by nothing.

📸 Studio milestone — before/after state of a Studio room unlocking, warm ambient animation

Outcomes

This is a design concept submitted for Phase 2 development evaluation, not a shipped product. But the business logic is tight.

D7
The discovery flow hooks at the exact moment of peak emotional receptivity. Players return for the shared weekly goal before they've consciously decided to stay.
D30
Arrows create identity-level attachment. A player who has contributed to three Studio room unlocks is not the same as a player with no investment. Leaving has a real, visible cost — without any pressure mechanic.
Ops
Zero moderation overhead. No chat. No free text. No reporting flow needed. The system is structurally incapable of producing the content that requires intervention.
📸 Business case diagram — D7/D30 retention loops, how key and arrow currencies drive each

What I'd Do Next

The Studio feed at low activity. When a Studio is quiet, the feed goes cold. A progressive reveal model helps, but the feed still needs a minimum viable signal even when members haven't been active. This is the main identified weakness from user testing.
Feedback tag visibility. Do tags feed back visibly to the designer's profile, or do they disappear into the algorithm? This question has UX and business implications and isn't resolved yet.
Permanent inventory monetization. Studio rooms unlock permanently, which creates real leaving cost. Whether that can also be a monetization surface (cosmetic Studio items, limited room decor) is a viable direction that's been deliberately parked for now.

Key Principles I'll Carry Forward

Social mechanics should be structurally incapable of producing toxicity, not reliant on settings or moderation to prevent bad outcomes.
The moment you ask someone to join, you create a moment where they can say no. Design the belonging, not the ask.
Absence must not feel consequential. Presence should feel meaningful. Those are not the same design problem.
